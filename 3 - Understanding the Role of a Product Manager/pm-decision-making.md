# PM Decision-Making Reflection

## 1. Research & Learning

### What is Feature Prioritisation?

Feature prioritisation is the process product managers use to decide which features should be built first and which should be delayed or rejected. Since development time and resources are limited, not every idea or request can be implemented immediately.

Product managers evaluate different factors to determine which features will provide the greatest value. Common prioritisation approaches include:

**Value vs Effort**

This method compares the expected value of a feature with the effort required to build it. Features with high value and low effort are usually prioritised.

**Impact vs Complexity**

PMs evaluate how much impact a feature will have on users or business goals compared to how complex it is to implement.

**RICE Framework**

The RICE model evaluates features based on four criteria:

- Reach – how many users the feature will affect
- Impact – how much the feature improves the user experience
- Confidence – how certain the team is about the expected impact
- Effort – the amount of work required to build it

These frameworks help PMs make structured decisions rather than relying only on intuition.

---

### Trade-offs Between User Needs, Technical Feasibility, and Business Impact

Product decisions usually involve balancing three important factors:

**User Needs**

Users may request new features or improvements based on their problems or frustrations. Solving real user problems is critical for product success.

**Technical Feasibility**

Some ideas may be valuable but difficult to implement due to technical limitations, system complexity, or development time.

**Business Impact**

A feature must also support business goals such as growth, retention, or product differentiation.

PMs must balance these factors carefully. A feature might be valuable for users but too expensive to build, or technically easy to build but not important enough to justify development resources.

---

### How PMs Decide What Not to Build

One of the most important responsibilities of a product manager is deciding what **not** to build.

Because development resources are limited, building every requested feature would lead to a complex product with scattered priorities. Instead, PMs must focus on features that align with the product vision and create meaningful value for users.

PMs may decide not to build a feature if:

- it does not solve an important user problem
- it does not align with the product strategy
- the development cost is too high compared to the benefit
- it introduces unnecessary complexity into the product

By rejecting or delaying low-impact ideas, the team can focus on building the features that matter most.

## 2. Reflection

### If Users Request a New Feature but It Is Costly to Develop

If users request a new feature that would be expensive or complex to build, the first step would be to understand the **underlying problem behind the request**.

Sometimes users suggest a specific feature, but what they actually need is a solution to a problem. By understanding the real problem, the team may be able to find a simpler or less expensive solution.

Next, I would evaluate:

- how many users are affected by the problem
- how severe the problem is
- whether the feature aligns with product priorities
- the estimated development effort

If the feature provides significant value for many users, it may still be worth building. However, if the cost is very high, the team may consider alternative solutions or delay the feature until it becomes more feasible.

---

### If Engineers Say a Feature Will Take Three Months Instead of One Month

If engineers estimate that a feature will take significantly longer than expected, I would first try to understand the reasons behind the increased complexity.

Possible questions include:

- What parts of the feature are technically difficult?
- Are there dependencies or architectural constraints?
- Can the feature be simplified?

After understanding the technical challenges, I would explore possible options with the team:

- breaking the feature into smaller deliverable parts
- releasing a simpler version first
- adjusting priorities in the roadmap
- delaying the feature if it no longer fits the timeline

This collaborative discussion helps ensure that product decisions remain realistic and aligned with development capacity.

---

### Why Saying “No” to Features Is Sometimes the Best Decision

Saying “no” to features is often necessary because product teams have limited time and resources. If every feature request were accepted, the product would quickly become complicated and difficult to maintain.

Good product management requires focus. By saying no to lower-impact ideas, the team can dedicate more time to features that provide real value to users.

In many cases, saying no is not about rejecting ideas permanently. It may simply mean postponing them until the product strategy, resources, or priorities change.

Ultimately, strong product decisions involve choosing the most meaningful improvements rather than trying to build everything at once.

---

## Final Reflection

Product management involves constant decision-making and balancing competing priorities. PMs must consider user needs, technical feasibility, and business impact while working closely with engineers, designers, and other stakeholders.

Learning how to prioritise effectively and make thoughtful trade-offs is essential for delivering valuable products. Understanding when to build something, and when not to, is a key skill that every product manager must develop.
