# Agile Principles & Frameworks

## 1. Research & Learning

### What is Agile?

Agile is a modern project management approach that focuses on flexibility, collaboration, and continuous improvement. Instead of following a rigid long-term plan, Agile teams work in smaller iterations and adapt their work based on feedback and changing requirements.

Agile encourages teams to deliver value incrementally, allowing products to evolve over time. This approach improves responsiveness to user needs and reduces the risk of delivering features that do not provide real value.

Traditional project management methods, such as the Waterfall model, follow a linear sequence of phases including planning, development, testing, and deployment. Once a phase is completed, it is difficult to go back and make changes.

In contrast, Agile allows teams to:

- adapt to changing requirements
- deliver smaller improvements frequently
- collaborate closely with stakeholders
- continuously improve their processes

This flexibility makes Agile particularly effective in software development and technology-driven environments.

---

### Core Values and Principles of Agile

The Agile approach is guided by the **Agile Manifesto**, which defines four core values:

**Individuals and interactions over processes and tools**

Agile teams prioritize communication and collaboration between team members rather than relying solely on rigid procedures or tools.

**Working software over comprehensive documentation**

Delivering functional software is more valuable than producing large amounts of documentation that may not contribute directly to the product.

**Customer collaboration over contract negotiation**

Agile encourages ongoing collaboration with customers and stakeholders to ensure the product meets real user needs.

**Responding to change over following a plan**

Agile teams are designed to adapt quickly when requirements or priorities change.

These values support the twelve Agile principles, which emphasize continuous delivery, sustainable development, close collaboration, and regular reflection to improve team performance.

### Differences Between Scrum and Kanban

Scrum and Kanban are two widely used Agile frameworks, but they differ in how work is organized and managed.

**Scrum**

Scrum organizes work into fixed time periods called sprints, usually lasting two to four weeks. During each sprint, the team commits to completing a set of tasks defined during Sprint Planning.

Scrum includes several structured ceremonies such as:

- Sprint Planning
- Daily Stand-up
- Sprint Review
- Sprint Retrospective

Scrum also defines specific roles including Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Development Team.

**Kanban**

Kanban focuses on visualizing workflow and managing work continuously rather than in fixed cycles. Tasks move through different workflow stages on a Kanban board, such as Backlog, In Progress, and Done.

Key characteristics of Kanban include:

- continuous workflow instead of fixed sprints
- visual task tracking using a Kanban board
- limiting work in progress (WIP)
- flexible task prioritization

While Scrum emphasizes structured planning cycles, Kanban focuses on optimizing workflow efficiency.

---

### Why Focus Bear Uses Kanban

Focus Bear tends to lean toward a Kanban-style workflow because it supports continuous development and flexible task management.

Kanban allows team members to work on tasks based on priority and capacity without waiting for the start of a new sprint. This is especially useful in environments where tasks can vary in size and urgency.

By visualizing tasks on a Kanban board, the team can easily track progress, identify bottlenecks, and adjust priorities when needed.

This flexibility helps maintain productivity and allows the team to respond quickly to changes.

# 2. Reflection

### Benefits and Challenges of Agile

Agile offers several important benefits for teams and organizations.

One of the biggest advantages is **flexibility**. Agile teams can adapt to changing requirements and continuously improve the product based on feedback.

Another key benefit is **transparency**. Agile workflows, such as Kanban boards, allow everyone in the team to see the status of tasks and understand project progress.

Agile also encourages **strong collaboration** between team members, which improves communication and problem-solving.

However, Agile can also present challenges. Without proper communication and discipline, tasks may become disorganized or priorities may change too frequently. Teams must maintain clear workflows and consistent communication to ensure Agile practices remain effective.

# 3. Task

### Main Differences Between Scrum and Kanban

In my understanding, the main difference between Scrum and Kanban lies in how work is organized and managed.

Scrum uses structured time-based cycles called sprints, where teams plan a set of tasks to complete within a fixed period. Scrum also includes defined roles and scheduled ceremonies that guide the development process.

Kanban, on the other hand, focuses on continuous workflow. Tasks move through a visual board based on their progress, and team members pull new tasks when they have capacity to work on them.

In simple terms:

- Scrum focuses on **structured sprint cycles and planning**
- Kanban focuses on **continuous task flow and workflow visualization**

Both frameworks aim to improve productivity, but they achieve this through different approaches.
