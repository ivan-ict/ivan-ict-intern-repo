## Cyber Security Awareness and Best Practices

### Common Cyber Security Threats in a Remote Work Environment

Remote work environments introduce several cyber security risks because employees access systems from different locations and networks. Some common threats include:

- **Phishing attacks** – malicious emails or messages designed to trick users into revealing sensitive information or clicking harmful links.
- **Credential theft** – attackers attempting to obtain login credentials through fake websites or social engineering.
- **Malware and ransomware** – malicious software that can steal data or lock systems until payment is made.
- **Unsecured networks** – using public Wi-Fi without protection may expose sensitive information.
- **Device theft or unauthorised access** – if devices are not properly locked or secured.

Because Focus Bear operates with remote team members, maintaining strong security habits is critical to protecting both company systems and user data.

---

## Best Practices for Securing Devices and Accounts

Several practices help maintain a strong level of cyber security:

- Keeping operating systems and software **regularly updated**
- Using **strong and unique passwords** for each account
- Enabling **two-factor authentication (2FA)** whenever possible
- Locking devices when stepping away from them
- Avoiding suspicious links or downloads
- Using trusted and secure internet connections

These practices help reduce the likelihood of unauthorised access to company accounts or systems.

---

## Importance of Locking Your Computer

Locking your computer when leaving your desk prevents others from accessing your accounts or sensitive information.

Even a short absence can create an opportunity for someone to access emails, internal systems, or confidential data. Automatic screen locking and password protection ensure that only authorised users can access the device.

This is especially important in shared environments such as co-working spaces, libraries, or public areas.

---

## Handling Phishing Attempts and Suspicious Links

Phishing attempts are one of the most common cyber security threats. These attacks often appear as emails or messages pretending to be from trusted organisations.

To handle phishing attempts safely:

- Always check the **sender’s email address carefully**
- Avoid clicking links from unknown or suspicious sources
- Be cautious of messages that create **urgency or pressure**
- Verify unusual requests through a **separate communication channel**
- Report suspicious messages to the appropriate team member

If something feels unusual or suspicious, it is safer to verify the message rather than responding immediately.

---

## Strong Passwords and Password Managers

A strong password should:

- Be **long (at least 12 characters)**
- Include a mix of **letters, numbers, and symbols**
- Avoid common words or personal information
- Be **unique for each account**

Using the same password across multiple services increases the risk of account compromise.

A **password manager** helps store and generate strong passwords securely. Instead of remembering many passwords, the user only needs to remember one master password.

Password managers also reduce the risk of reusing weak passwords across multiple accounts.

---

## Importance of Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Two-factor authentication adds an extra layer of security to accounts.

With 2FA enabled, logging into an account requires:

1. Something you **know** (your password)
2. Something you **have** (such as a phone verification code or authentication app)

Even if an attacker obtains your password, they cannot access the account without the second authentication factor.

2FA should always be enabled for:

- Email accounts
- Work collaboration tools
- Cloud services
- Payment systems
- Developer or product management tools
